    Final Project: Independent GIS Analysis

    Learning Goals

    By the end of this project, you should be able to:

    1. Use GIS to solve a practical problem of your own design
    2. Collect or access geospatial data
    3. Analyze the collected data using appropriate geoprocessing operations
    4. Present your findings in a written report complete with maps and statistics

    Project Timeline

    Phase 1: Brainstorm

    Come up with your own geospatial analysis idea

    Phase 2: Check-ins

    Meet with professor/TA for feedback and direction

    Phase 3: Outline

    Develop detailed project plan

    Phase 4: Execution

    Conduct analysis and create deliverables

    Phase 5: Presentation (Optional)

    Present findings to class for extra credit

    Phase 1: Final Project Brainstorm

    Assignment

    Submit a well thought-out paragraph describing:

    1. The question/problem you have identified
    2. The geographic data you will use to answer the question
    3. The geoprocessing steps you will perform on the data
    4. The expected outputs of your analysis

    Alternative: Frame your question as a hypothesis test (as in the Scientific Method)

    Project Ideas to Get Started

    Personal Tracking: - Record your hikes/runs/bike rides over the quarter (using Strava) - Plot your routes on a map - Analyze patterns, distances, elevation gains

    Climate Analysis: - Analyze satellite-based rainfall data in SLO County - What parts get most/least rain? - Can you explain why?

    Change Detection: - Determine area of recently burned fire - Analyze changes in satellite imagery (before/after) - Calculate burn severity

    Urban Analysis: - Map coffee shop accessibility in your neighborhood - Identify food deserts - Analyze public transit coverage

    Environmental: - Model habitat suitability for a species - Analyze vegetation health using NDVI - Map stream water quality

    Social: - Analyze demographic patterns - Map access to healthcare - Study gentrification patterns

    Submission Requirements

    Submit: 1 paragraph (150-250 words) including: - Problem description - Datasets to be used - Processing techniques - Expected results

    Phase 2: Final Project Check-Ins

    Purpose

    The professor and TA will provide directed feedback based on your brainstorm submission.

    Meeting topics: - Set direction - Select datasets - Explore processing techniques - Scope appropriately

    Before Check-In

    STRONGLY RECOMMENDED: - Work on project between brainstorm and check-in - Download sample data - Set realistic scope - Test basic workflows

    This ensures project remains on track!

    Phase 3: Final Project Outline

    Assignment

    Based on feedback from conversations and written comments, write a one-page outline describing your study.

    Format

    1. Introduction

    What problem are you trying to solve?

    Consider: - Why is it important? - Why does it matter to you and/or the world? - Frame as a question

    Example questions: - Are different demographic groups disproportionately exposed to poor air quality? - How are temperature and precipitation patterns changing in SLO County? - What's the fastest route I can bike between landmarks on campus? How does bike infrastructure/time of day factor in?

    IMPORTANT: Do not choose: - Too challenging a problem - Too large a geographic area - Too long a time window

    You have 5 weeks - make it well-scoped and achievable!

    2. Data

    What data will you use?

    Describe: - Is this primary or secondary data? - What formats (shapefile, raster, CSV)? - Where will you get it? - What resolution/coverage?

    IMPORTANT: - Do not wait until the last minute to download data! - Strongly suggest finding and downloading sample data WITH this submission - This will guide scope of analysis

    3. Methods

    Secondary data: - What geoprocessing or analyses will you apply? - What tools will you use? - What is the workflow?

    Primary data: - How will you collect it? - What software will be used? - Can you upload it into GIS?

    4. Expected Conclusions/Hypotheses

    What do you expect to see? - What patterns might emerge? - What results would support your hypothesis? - What might surprise you?

    Submission Requirements

    Submit: One-page outline (PDF or Word) with all 4 sections

    Phase 4: Final Project Report

    Report Requirements

    Submit an 8-12 page report with the following sections:

    A. Introduction

    Describe the problem/question

    Include: - Background and context - Why this matters - Research question(s) - Scope and limitations

    Length: 1-2 pages

    B. Data and Methods

    What data did you use?

    For each dataset, describe: - Source (where obtained) - Type (vector/raster/tabular) - Resolution/scale - Date/temporal coverage - Coordinate system - Limitations

    What processing steps did you perform?

    Include: - Workflow diagram - Tools used - Parameters chosen - Why you made these choices

    Length: 2-3 pages

    C. Results

    Describe the results of the analysis

    Include: - Maps (multiple!) - Charts and graphs - Statistics and tables - Clear figure captions - Reference figures in text

    Organize logically: - Present findings systematically - Build from simple to complex - Lead reader through analysis

    Length: 3-4 pages

    D. Discussion

    Discuss and contextualize the results

    Address: - Were results expected? Why or why not? - What assumptions were made? - How do results recontextualize the original question? - What are limitations of the analysis? - What further analysis would you perform? - How do results compare to other studies?

    Be critical: - Acknowledge uncertainties - Discuss potential errors - Consider alternative explanations

    Length: 2-3 pages

    E. Conclusions

    High-level takeaways

    Include: - Summary of main findings - Significance of results - What would you do differently? - Future work suggestions - Broader implications

    Length: 1 page

    F. References

    Citations and original sources

    Include: - Data sources (with URLs and access dates) - Websites used - Publications referenced - Any other sources

    Format: Harvard style (or another consistent format)

    Report Formatting Guidelines

    Structure: - 12-pt font (Times New Roman or Arial) - 1-inch margins - Double-spaced text - Single-spaced captions - Page numbers - Section headers

    Figures: - High-quality maps (300 dpi) - Clear, readable charts - Descriptive captions - Referenced in text

    Writing: - Professional tone - Clear and concise - Proofread carefully - Avoid jargon (or define it)

    Phase 5: Final Project Presentation [Optional - Extra Credit]

    Presentation Requirements

    Format: 10-minute presentation to class

    Structure (mirror report):

    1. Title slide (30 sec)
    2. Project title

    3. Your name

    4. Introduction (2 min)

    5. Problem/question
    6. Why it matters

    7. Research objectives

    8. Data & Methods (2 min)

    9. Data sources
    10. Workflow diagram

    11. Key processing steps

    12. Results (4 min)

    13. Maps created
    14. Key findings
    15. Figures, graphs, charts

    16. Statistics

    17. Conclusions (1.5 min)

    18. Main takeaways
    19. Limitations
    20. Future work

    Total: 10 minutes + 2-3 minutes for questions

    Presentation Tips

    Visual aids: - Clear, large maps - Simple, readable charts - Limited text on slides - High-quality graphics

    Delivery: - Practice timing - Speak clearly - Make eye contact - Explain technical terms - Engage audience

    Content: - Tell a story - Focus on key points - Skip minor details - Emphasize findings

    Grading Rubric

    Written Report (100 points)

    Component Points
    Introduction
    Problem clearly defined 5 pts
    Context and significance 5 pts
    Data & Methods
    Data sources documented 10 pts
    Methods clearly explained 10 pts
    Workflow diagram included 5 pts
    Results
    Maps (quality and quantity) 15 pts
    Charts/graphs 10 pts
    Clear presentation 10 pts
    Discussion
    Interpretation of results 10 pts
    Critical analysis 5 pts
    Limitations acknowledged 5 pts
    Conclusions
    Summary of findings 5 pts
    Future directions 3 pts
    Writing Quality
    Organization 5 pts
    Clarity 4 pts
    Grammar/spelling 3 pts
    References
    Complete citations 5 pts
    TOTAL 100 pts

    Presentation (10 extra credit points)

    Component Points
    Content clarity 4 pts
    Visual aids quality 3 pts
    Delivery 2 pts
    Timing 1 pt

    Project Scoping Guidelines

    Good Project Scope

    Clear research question Defined geographic area (neighborhood to county scale) Limited time period (1 year to 1 decade) Available data (free, accessible) Achievable in 5 weeks (with weekly progress) Demonstrates GIS skills (multiple tools used)

    Too Ambitious

    Vague question ("Study climate change") Huge area (entire continent) Long time series (100 years of data) Data doesn't exist (or is very expensive) Too complex (would take months) Minimal GIS (mostly manual work)

    Just Right Examples

    "How has urban development affected tree canopy cover in SLO (2010-2024)?"

    "Where are the best locations for new bike lanes in downtown based on current usage and destinations?"

    "What is the relationship between elevation and rainfall in the SLO watershed?"

    Common Mistakes to Avoid

    Waiting too long to start Scope creep - adding more and more Data issues discovered late Poor documentation of methods Low-quality maps - missing elements Weak discussion - just restating results No critical analysis - acknowledging limitations Incomplete references Ignoring feedback from check-ins

    • Refine maps and figures
    • Start writing draft

    Week 5

    • Create final maps
    • Proofread
    • Submit report

    Final Thoughts

    This is your project - make it something you care about!

    Best projects: - Address real problems - Use thoughtful analysis - Produce actionable results - Are well-documented - Tell a clear story

    This is your chance to: - Apply everything you've learned - Explore your interests - Build your portfolio - Practice real-world GIS

    Make it count!

    Questions?

    See instructor during: - Office hours - Lab sessions - Email for appointment

    Start early, work consistently, ask for help!

    Good luck!
