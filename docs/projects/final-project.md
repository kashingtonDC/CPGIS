# Final Project: Independent GIS Analysis

## Learning Goals

By the end of this project, you should be able to:

1. Use GIS to solve a practical problem of your own design
2. Collect or access geospatial data
3. Analyze the collected data using appropriate geoprocessing operations
4. Present your findings in a written report complete with maps and statistics

---

## Project Timeline

### Phase 1: Brainstorm
Come up with your own geospatial analysis idea

### Phase 2: Check-ins
Meet with professor/TA for feedback and direction

### Phase 3: Outline
Develop detailed project plan

### Phase 4: Execution
Conduct analysis and create deliverables

### Phase 5: Presentation (Optional)
Present findings to class for extra credit

## Phase 1: Final Project Brainstorm

### Assignment

Submit a **well thought-out paragraph** describing:

1. **The question/problem** you have identified
2. **The geographic data** you will use to answer the question
3. **The geoprocessing steps** you will perform on the data
4. **The expected outputs** of your analysis

**Alternative**: Frame your question as a **hypothesis test** (as in the Scientific Method)

### Project Ideas to Get Started

**Personal Tracking**:
- Record your hikes/runs/bike rides over the quarter (using Strava)
- Plot your routes on a map
- Analyze patterns, distances, elevation gains

**Climate Analysis**:
- Analyze satellite-based rainfall data in SLO County
- What parts get most/least rain?
- Can you explain why?

**Change Detection**:
- Determine area of recently burned fire
- Analyze changes in satellite imagery (before/after)
- Calculate burn severity

**Urban Analysis**:
- Map coffee shop accessibility in your neighborhood
- Identify food deserts
- Analyze public transit coverage

**Environmental**:
- Model habitat suitability for a species
- Analyze vegetation health using NDVI
- Map stream water quality

**Social**:
- Analyze demographic patterns
- Map access to healthcare
- Study gentrification patterns

### Submission Requirements

**Submit**: 1 paragraph (150-250 words) including:
- Problem description
- Datasets to be used
- Processing techniques
- Expected results

## Phase 3: Final Project Outline

### Assignment

Based on feedback from conversations and written comments, write a **one-page outline** describing your study.

### Format

**1. Introduction**

**What problem are you trying to solve?**

**Consider**:
- Why is it important?
- Why does it matter to you and/or the world?
- Frame as a question

**Example questions**:
- Are different demographic groups disproportionately exposed to poor air quality?
- How are temperature and precipitation patterns changing in SLO County?
- What's the fastest route I can bike between landmarks on campus? How does bike infrastructure/time of day factor in?

‚ö†Ô∏è **IMPORTANT**: Do not choose:
- Too challenging a problem
- Too large a geographic area
- Too long a time window

**You have 5 weeks** - make it well-scoped and achievable!

---

**2. Data**

**What data will you use?**

**Describe**:
- Is this primary or secondary data?
- What formats (shapefile, raster, CSV)?
- Where will you get it?
- What resolution/coverage?

‚ö†Ô∏è **IMPORTANT**: 
- **Do not wait until the last minute** to download data!
- **Strongly suggest** finding and downloading sample data WITH this submission
- This will guide scope of analysis

---

**3. Methods**

**Secondary data**:
- What geoprocessing or analyses will you apply?
- What tools will you use?
- What is the workflow?

**Primary data**:
- How will you collect it?
- What software will be used?
- Can you upload it into GIS?

---

**4. Expected Conclusions/Hypotheses**

**What do you expect to see?**
- What patterns might emerge?
- What results would support your hypothesis?
- What might surprise you?

### Submission Requirements

**Submit**: One-page outline (PDF or Word) with all 4 sections

## Phase 4: Final Project Report

### Report Requirements

Submit an **8-12 page report** with the following sections:

---

### A. Introduction

**Describe the problem/question**

**Include**:
- Background and context
- Why this matters
- Research question(s)
- Scope and limitations

**Length**: 1-2 pages

---

### B. Data and Methods

**What data did you use?**

**For each dataset, describe**:
- Source (where obtained)
- Type (vector/raster/tabular)
- Resolution/scale
- Date/temporal coverage
- Coordinate system
- Limitations

**What processing steps did you perform?**

**Include**:
- Workflow diagram
- Tools used
- Parameters chosen
- Why you made these choices

**Length**: 2-3 pages

---

### C. Results

**Describe the results of the analysis**

**Include**:
- Maps (multiple!)
- Charts and graphs
- Statistics and tables
- Clear figure captions
- Reference figures in text

**Organize logically**:
- Present findings systematically
- Build from simple to complex
- Lead reader through analysis

**Length**: 3-4 pages

---

### D. Discussion

**Discuss and contextualize the results**

**Address**:
- Were results expected? Why or why not?
- What assumptions were made?
- How do results recontextualize the original question?
- What are limitations of the analysis?
- What further analysis would you perform?
- How do results compare to other studies?

**Be critical**:
- Acknowledge uncertainties
- Discuss potential errors
- Consider alternative explanations

**Length**: 2-3 pages

---

### E. Conclusions

**High-level takeaways**

**Include**:
- Summary of main findings
- Significance of results
- What would you do differently?
- Future work suggestions
- Broader implications

**Length**: 1 page

---

### F. References

**Citations and original sources**

**Include**:
- Data sources (with URLs and access dates)
- Websites used
- Publications referenced
- Any other sources

**Format**: Harvard style (or another consistent format)

---

### Report Formatting Guidelines

**Structure**:
- 12-pt font (Times New Roman or Arial)
- 1-inch margins
- Double-spaced text
- Single-spaced captions
- Page numbers
- Section headers

**Figures**:
- High-quality maps (300 dpi)
- Clear, readable charts
- Descriptive captions
- Referenced in text

**Writing**:
- Professional tone
- Clear and concise
- Proofread carefully
- Avoid jargon (or define it)

---

## Phase 5: Final Project Presentation [Optional - Extra Credit]

### Presentation Requirements

**Format**: 10-minute presentation to class

**Structure** (mirror report):

1. **Title slide** (30 sec)
   - Project title
   - Your name

2. **Introduction** (2 min)
   - Problem/question
   - Why it matters
   - Research objectives

3. **Data & Methods** (2 min)
   - Data sources
   - Workflow diagram
   - Key processing steps

4. **Results** (4 min)
   - Maps created
   - Key findings
   - Figures, graphs, charts
   - Statistics

5. **Conclusions** (1.5 min)
   - Main takeaways
   - Limitations
   - Future work

**Total**: 10 minutes + 2-3 minutes for questions

---

### Presentation Tips

**Visual aids**:
- Clear, large maps
- Simple, readable charts
- Limited text on slides
- High-quality graphics

**Delivery**:
- Practice timing
- Speak clearly
- Make eye contact
- Explain technical terms
- Engage audience

**Content**:
- Tell a story
- Focus on key points
- Skip minor details
- Emphasize findings

## Project Scoping Guidelines

### Good Project Scope

‚úÖ **Clear research question**
‚úÖ **Defined geographic area** (neighborhood to county scale)
‚úÖ **Limited time period** (1 year to 1 decade)
‚úÖ **Available data** (free, accessible)
‚úÖ **Achievable in 5 weeks** (with weekly progress)
‚úÖ **Demonstrates GIS skills** (multiple tools used)

### Too Ambitious

‚ùå **Vague question** ("Study climate change")
‚ùå **Huge area** (entire continent)
‚ùå **Long time series** (100 years of data)
‚ùå **Data doesn't exist** (or is very expensive)
‚ùå **Too complex** (would take months)
‚ùå **Minimal GIS** (mostly manual work)

### Just Right Examples

‚úÖ "How has urban development affected tree canopy cover in SLO (2010-2024)?"

‚úÖ "Where are the best locations for new bike lanes in downtown based on current usage and destinations?"

‚úÖ "What is the relationship between elevation and rainfall in the SLO watershed?"

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

‚ùå **Waiting too long** to start
‚ùå **Scope creep** - adding more and more
‚ùå **Data issues** discovered late
‚ùå **Poor documentation** of methods
‚ùå **Low-quality maps** - missing elements
‚ùå **Weak discussion** - just restating results
‚ùå **No critical analysis** - acknowledging limitations
‚ùå **Incomplete references**
‚ùå **Ignoring feedback** from check-ins
